Three Fundamental Questions

Before evaluating any explanation, ask these three questions. A good explanation must satisfy all three.

  1. Is it truthful? Does the explanation accurately reflect what the model actually does?
  2. Is it understandable? Can the intended audience make sense of it?
  3. Is it useful? Does it support the decisions or actions the user needs to take?

Properties of Good Explanations

Miller (2019) identifies key properties that explanations should have, drawn from the social science literature on how humans explain and understand causality.

Property Meaning Example Related Methods
Causal Cites causes, not correlations — the explanation identifies what actually drives the outcome. "Your loan was denied because your debt-to-income ratio exceeds the threshold." Counterfactual Explanations
Contrastive Answers "Why A rather than B?" — humans naturally explain by contrasting what happened with what could have happened. "You were rejected (A) rather than accepted (B) because your income was $5k too low." Counterfactual Explanations
Selective Highlights only 1–2 key causes rather than an exhaustive list — cognitive load requires brevity. Presenting the top 2 feature contributions instead of all features. LIME (via regularization) — NOT SHAP
Social Tailored to the audience — a good explanation adapts its depth, vocabulary, and framing to who is receiving it. A clinician receives a different explanation than a patient for the same model prediction. All methods (requires design choice)

Truthfulness

Truthfulness is the most fundamental requirement: an explanation must faithfully represent the model's behaviour. Three dimensions characterise truthfulness.

Dimension Meaning
Coverage The proportion of instances for which the explanation applies. A rule covering only 2% of cases is not a general truth about the model.
Expressive power How rich a class of explanations can be expressed. A linear surrogate cannot capture interactions; a decision tree can.
Fidelity How closely the explanation's predictions match those of the original model. High fidelity means the explanation is an accurate proxy; low fidelity means it misrepresents the model.

Three Evaluation Levels

Doshi-Velez & Kim (2017) propose three levels at which XAI methods can be evaluated, forming a hierarchy from the most ecologically valid (but expensive) to the most controlled (but cheap).

Level Description Ecological Validity Internal Validity Cost
Application Grounded Domain experts perform real tasks using the explanation in the actual deployment context. High Lower High
Human Grounded Lay humans perform simplified tasks in a controlled setting (e.g., forward simulation, binary forced-choice). Moderate Moderate Moderate
Functionally Grounded Formal proxy metrics are used with no human participants (e.g., fidelity, complexity, consistency). Low High Low
Key Trade-off

Ecological validity (how realistic the evaluation setting is) vs Internal validity (how well-controlled and replicable the evaluation is). Application Grounded evaluations are the gold standard for deployment decisions but are expensive and difficult to replicate. Functionally Grounded evaluations are cheap and reproducible but may not predict real human performance.

Key Research Findings

Kaur et al. — Overtrust in XAI Tools

Even data scientists overuse, misuse, and underuse XAI tools. Kaur et al. found that practitioners often accept SHAP explanations uncritically, over-trust them without verifying, and dismiss counterintuitive results as "complex math" — a form of confirmation bias that can lead to dangerous false confidence in model behaviour.

  • Counterfactual explanations are human-friendly because they are contrastive — matching how humans naturally reason about "why A rather than B?" without requiring knowledge of model internals.
  • SHAP is NOT inherently selective: it assigns a value to every feature. Selectivity must be imposed externally (e.g., displaying only the top-$k$ values), meaning it does not satisfy Miller's selectivity property by design.
  • LIME regularization → selectivity, NOT consistency: LIME's $\ell_1$ regularization enforces sparse (selective) explanations, but because LIME samples a new neighbourhood each run, explanations for the same instance can differ — it trades consistency for selectivity.
  • Global surrogates often have lower fidelity than local surrogates: a single global surrogate model must approximate the black box over the entire input space, whereas a local surrogate (like LIME) only needs to be accurate in a small neighbourhood, making local fidelity easier to achieve.
