Four Questions Framework

When evaluating any claim or piece of information, apply these four questions to assess its credibility and intent.

  1. Who is telling you this? — Assess the credibility of the source: their expertise, track record, and potential conflicts of interest.
  2. Why are they telling you? — Identify the agenda: what does the source stand to gain from your believing this claim?
  3. Where does the info come from? — Trace the data source: is the underlying evidence primary, secondary, or anecdotal? Can it be verified independently?
  4. What are they actually claiming? — Examine the precision of language: are terms defined carefully? Are qualifiers (always, never, most, some) used accurately?

Misleading Info Taxonomy

Not all misleading information is the same. The key dimensions are whether the content is false and whether the falsehood is intentional.

Term False? Intentional?
Misinformation Yes No
Disinformation Yes Yes
Fake news Yes Either
Propaganda Can be true or false Yes

Correlation vs Causation

Warning

Correlation does NOT imply causation. Confounding variables create spurious correlations that can appear statistically significant while reflecting no genuine causal relationship.

Example: Ice cream sales and drowning rates are positively correlated. The apparent relationship is entirely explained by a confounding variable — hot weather increases both ice cream consumption and swimming activity, and therefore drowning risk. Ice cream does not cause drowning.

Connection to Part 1

Historical bias in data can reflect correlations mistaken for causal relationships — a model trained on such data perpetuates the bias.

Visualization Tricks

Misleading data visualizations exploit perceptual and cognitive shortcuts. Recognizing these tricks is essential for critical evaluation of charts and graphs.

Trick Problem Rule
Truncated y-axis Exaggerates differences between values by cutting off the bottom of the axis Proportional ink principle
Inverted axes Increases look like decreases (and vice versa), reversing the visual narrative Check axis direction
Dual y-axes Manipulated scales on two independent axes suggest false correlations between unrelated series Be skeptical of dual-axis charts
3D effects Perspective distorts the perceived size of bars or slices, making some values appear larger than they are Avoid unnecessary 3D
Cherry-picked time ranges Selecting a convenient start or end date can support any desired narrative while hiding contradicting trends Check the full time series
Misleading maps Area does not equal population: large, sparsely populated regions dominate choropleth maps visually Use cartograms for population data
Example: Florida gun deaths and the inverted y-axis

A widely circulated chart published after Florida's "Stand Your Ground" law was passed displayed gun death counts on an inverted y-axis: zero was at the top of the chart and higher values appeared lower on the page. The line in the chart dramatically fell just after the law took effect in 2005, visually implying that gun deaths had decreased. In reality, deaths were increasing — the downward visual trend was an artifact of the flipped axis. The chart is a canonical example of how axis manipulation can reverse the narrative of a dataset without altering a single data point.

Key Insight

The principle of proportional ink: bar charts must start at zero. The amount of ink (or pixels) used to represent a value should be directly proportional to the value itself. Truncating the y-axis violates this principle and systematically exaggerates visual differences.

AI & Disinformation

Generative AI raises four specific fears about the future of disinformation at scale.

  1. Quantity — AI enables the production of misleading content at a massive scale with minimal human effort, flooding information ecosystems.
  2. Quality — Generated text, images, audio, and video are increasingly convincing, making deepfakes and synthetic misinformation harder to detect.
  3. Personalization — AI allows disinformation to be precisely targeted and personalized to individual users, exploiting known psychological vulnerabilities and filter bubbles.
  4. Involuntary generation (hallucinations) — Even without malicious intent, AI systems produce false information confidently. These hallucinations can spread as misinformation without any deliberate human actor.
Counterarguments: reasons AI disinformation risk may be overstated

Counterarguments: (1) Human attention is finite — there's a limit to how much disinformation people can consume. (2) Persuasion is inherently difficult — most people don't change views from a single piece of content. (3) Marginal risk over existing disinformation may be small — humans already produce vast amounts of misleading content.

Brandolini's Law

Brandolini's Law (Bullshit Asymmetry Principle)

"The energy needed to refute bullshit is an order of magnitude bigger than to produce it."

Formulated by Alberto Brandolini (2013). The asymmetry arises because producing a false or misleading claim requires no supporting evidence, while refuting it demands sourcing primary evidence, checking methodology, explaining context, and communicating nuance — all of which take substantially more effort to do convincingly than the original falsehood required.

Exercises

Exercise 10.1 — A news article shows a bar chart: Party A at 48%, Party B at 52%. Y-axis starts at 45%. Is this misleading?

Yes. Starting at 45% instead of 0% dramatically exaggerates the visual difference. Party B appears ~3x as much as Party A, when the actual difference is only 4 percentage points. Violates the principle of proportional ink.

Exercise 10.2 — A study finds countries with more chocolate consumption per capita have more Nobel Prize winners. What should you conclude?

Classic correlation ≠ causation fallacy. Confounding variables (GDP, education funding, research infrastructure) likely correlate with both chocolate consumption and Nobel Prizes.

Exercise 10.3 — A politician shares an article claiming a policy will create 1 million jobs. The politician knows the study used flawed methodology but shares it anyway. Classify this.

Disinformation — the information is false (flawed methodology) and shared intentionally. Also classifiable as propaganda (promoting a viewpoint regardless of truth).
