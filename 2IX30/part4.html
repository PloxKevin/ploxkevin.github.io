EU Non-Discrimination Law

EU anti-discrimination law (e.g. Employment Equality Directive 2000/78/EC, Equal Treatment Directive) prohibits discrimination on protected grounds such as race, sex, religion, age, and disability. Two key legal concepts apply.

Definition — Direct Discrimination

One person is treated less favourably than another has been, is, or would be treated in a comparable situation, on grounds of a protected characteristic (e.g. race, sex, age). Intent does not need to be proven.

Definition — Indirect Discrimination

An apparently neutral provision, criterion or practice puts persons sharing a protected characteristic at a particular disadvantage compared with others — unless that provision is objectively justified by a legitimate aim and the means of achieving it are appropriate and necessary.

Key Insight

Satisfying a fairness metric does NOT imply legal compliance. Fairness metrics do not meet the evidential requirements for establishing discrimination under EU law. Legal proceedings require context-specific causal analysis and comparator groups — not aggregate statistical parity scores.

Important Note

EU law is NOT a decision tree — it requires contextual reasoning about legitimate aims and proportionality.

Four-Fifths Rule

A practical heuristic (originating from US EEOC guidelines) used to detect potential adverse impact in selection procedures. A selection rate below 80 % of the rate for the most-favoured group may indicate discrimination.

$$\frac{\text{Selection rate of deprived group}}{\text{Selection rate of favoured group}} \geq 0.8$$
Limitation

The 0.8 threshold is arbitrary and provides no contextual justification. A ratio of 0.79 and 0.81 are treated very differently despite being nearly identical. The rule ignores sample size, base rates, and the broader sociotechnical context of the selection process.

Five Abstraction Traps

Identified by Selbst et al. (2019), these are pitfalls that arise when fairness is addressed in isolation from the broader sociotechnical system. Each trap represents a failure of abstraction when moving between the technical and social layers.

Trap Definition Key Question
Framing Trap Not including all relevant parts of the sociotechnical system in the problem definition. The ML model is treated as if it operates in isolation. Have we defined the problem to include all stakeholders and system components?
Portability Trap Assuming that a fairness solution or its requirements can be transferred intact from one social or institutional context to another without adjustment. Does this solution make assumptions about context that do not hold here?
Formalism Trap Oversimplifying rich social concepts (e.g. fairness, discrimination) into a single mathematical metric, losing the nuance and contestedness of the underlying concept. What is lost when we reduce this social concept to a number?
Ripple Effect Trap Failing to see how deploying a new technology changes existing social dynamics and incentives. Related to Goodhart's Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. How will optimising for this metric change the behaviour of people in the system?
Solutionism Trap Assuming that a machine learning model is the right solution to the problem, rather than asking whether a technical intervention is appropriate at all. Should this problem be solved with ML, or are non-technical interventions more appropriate?

Abstraction Traps in Practice

Trap Case Study Connection
Framing Resume screening in multi-stage hiring Fairness of model ≠ fairness of system
Portability Google Gemini diversity constraints on historical images Requirements don’t transfer across contexts
Formalism Equating “fairness” with a single metric DP alone ≠ true fairness
Ripple Effect Overtrust after deploying XAI tools Goodhart’s Law
Solutionism Building ML when the real problem is poor management Not every problem needs ML

Ethics Framework

Three major ethical frameworks provide different lenses for evaluating fairness decisions in ML systems.

Framework Philosopher(s) Core Principle Applied to Fairness in ML
Deontology Immanuel Kant Morality is grounded in rules and duties. An act is right if it conforms to a universal moral law (the Categorical Imperative), regardless of consequences. Certain uses of protected attributes may be inherently wrong, irrespective of outcome. Rights must not be violated even for aggregate benefit.
Utilitarianism John Stuart Mill, Jeremy Bentham An act is right if it produces the greatest good for the greatest number. Consequences (welfare, utility) are what matter morally. A system causing slight harm to a few may be justified if aggregate welfare is maximised. Tension with individual rights.
Virtue Ethics Aristotle Morality is about developing good character and virtues (e.g. justice, honesty, courage). The right act is what a virtuous person would do. Focuses on the practitioner: are data scientists acting with integrity, practical wisdom (phronesis), and genuine concern for those affected?

Egalitarianism & Equality of Opportunity

Philosophical conceptions of equality of opportunity motivate different fairness metrics. The two main views lead directly to different technical choices.

Formal Equality of Opportunity (EoO)

Individuals should be judged only on characteristics that are relevant to the task — a meritocratic ideal. Irrelevant characteristics (race, sex, etc.) should not affect decisions. This aligns with Equalized Odds (equal TPR and FPR across groups): similarly qualified individuals should receive similar outcomes.

Substantive Equality of Opportunity (EoO)

Individuals with the same innate potential should face the same prospects, regardless of circumstances of birth (family, class, social environment). Past disadvantage must be corrected. This aligns with Demographic Parity: outcomes should be distributed equally across protected groups, regardless of intermediate qualifications that may themselves reflect structural disadvantage.

Takeaway

The choice of fairness metric is not merely technical — it encodes a political and philosophical position on what equality means. Neither formal nor substantive EoO is objectively correct; the right choice depends on context, law, and values.

Exercises

Exercise 4.1 — Framing Trap in Hiring

A hiring ML model satisfies demographic parity. Resume selection is one step in a multi-stage hiring process. How might evaluating fairness through metrics alone cause the framing trap?

Show Answer

By measuring fairness purely in terms of model output metrics, we fail to model the entire sociotechnical system. Subsequent stages (interviews, reference checks, final decisions) may introduce additional bias. The framing trap occurs because we define the boundary of our fairness analysis too narrowly — focusing only on the ML model rather than the full hiring pipeline.

Exercise 4.2 — Google Gemini Case

Google Gemini applied diversity constraints to historical figures in image generation. Which abstraction trap does this represent?

Show Answer

Portability trap. Requirements from one context (producing diverse representation in contemporary images) were incorrectly transferred to another context (historical images), where the same constraints produce factually inaccurate results.

Exercise 4.3 — Accuracy vs. Demographic Parity

It is hard to achieve high accuracy and satisfy demographic parity when:

  • (a) Attributes correlate with both $A$ and $Y$
  • (b) Irrelevant attributes correlate with $A$
  • (c) Relevant attributes don’t correlate with $A$
Show Answer

(a). If attributes correlate with both $A$ and $Y$ (historical bias encoded in data), a perfectly accurate classifier cannot satisfy demographic parity — to be accurate it must use group-correlated features, but this produces group-disparate predictions.

Practice Problems

Problem 10 — Abstraction Traps

For each scenario, identify which abstraction trap (Framing, Portability, Formalism, Ripple Effect, Solutionism) applies and explain why.

(a) Managers over-rely on ML recommendations and stop conducting interviews themselves

Ripple Effect trap. Deploying the ML tool changed human behavior in the system — managers now use the model as a crutch rather than as a decision support tool. This is an instance of Goodhart’s Law: optimizing the metric (model performance) changed the broader social dynamics.

(b) A team measures demographic parity and declares the system “fair”

Formalism trap. Reducing “fairness” to a single mathematical metric (DP) loses the social nuance of the concept. The system may still be unfair in terms of equalized odds, individual fairness, or substantive equality — none of which are captured by DP alone.

(c) A fraud detection model trained in the Netherlands is deployed in Brazil without modification

Portability trap. The model was trained under assumptions about the Dutch social and financial context. Those assumptions (population demographics, fraud patterns, legal definitions) do not transfer to Brazil, so the fairness and accuracy properties of the model cannot be assumed to hold.

(d) An organization builds an ML model to predict employee performance when the real problem is poor management practices

Solutionism trap. The organization assumes that a technical ML intervention is the right solution, when the root cause is a social/organizational problem (poor management). Non-technical interventions (management training, HR policy reform) may be more appropriate and effective.

(e) A loan approval model is evaluated for fairness without considering how approved loans lead to downstream financial decisions

Framing trap. The boundary of the fairness analysis is drawn too narrowly around the loan approval model, ignoring downstream decisions (loan terms, interest rates, servicing practices) that may introduce further disparate impact.
