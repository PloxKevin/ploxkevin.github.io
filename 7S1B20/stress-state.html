1. 3D Stress Tensor

The complete state of stress at a point in a solid body is described by the Cauchy stress tensor — a 3×3 matrix containing all normal and shear stress components acting on three mutually perpendicular faces.

Stress Tensor at a Point
$$\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{xx} & \sigma_{xy} & \sigma_{xz} \\ \sigma_{yx} & \sigma_{yy} & \sigma_{yz} \\ \sigma_{zx} & \sigma_{zy} & \sigma_{zz} \end{bmatrix}$$ The diagonal components $\sigma_{xx}$, $\sigma_{yy}$, $\sigma_{zz}$ are normal stresses. The off-diagonal components $\sigma_{xy}$, $\sigma_{xz}$, $\sigma_{yz}$ are shear stresses.

Symmetry of the Stress Tensor

By considering moment equilibrium of an infinitesimal volume element, it can be shown that the stress tensor is symmetric:

$$\sigma_{xy} = \sigma_{yx}, \qquad \sigma_{xz} = \sigma_{zx}, \qquad \sigma_{yz} = \sigma_{zy}$$

This symmetry reduces the number of independent stress components from 9 to 6: three normal stresses $(\sigma_{xx}, \sigma_{yy}, \sigma_{zz})$ and three shear stresses $(\sigma_{xy}, \sigma_{xz}, \sigma_{yz})$.

Key insight
The symmetry $\sigma_{ij} = \sigma_{ji}$ follows from angular momentum balance — if shear stresses on opposite faces were not equal and opposite, the volume element would have a net moment and spin indefinitely. Physically: the shear on the $x$-face in the $y$-direction must equal the shear on the $y$-face in the $x$-direction.

Sign Convention

A stress component $\sigma_{ij}$ is positive when:

  • It acts on a face with outward normal in the positive $i$-direction, and points in the positive $j$-direction, or
  • It acts on a face with outward normal in the negative $i$-direction, and points in the negative $j$-direction.

Normal stresses are positive in tension, negative in compression.

2. Cauchy's Formula

Given any plane through a point, specified by its unit outward normal $\mathbf{n} = [n_x, n_y, n_z]^T$, the traction vector (stress vector) on that plane is:

Cauchy's Formula
$$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \boldsymbol{\sigma}\,\mathbf{n}$$ In component form: $$\begin{bmatrix} t_x \\ t_y \\ t_z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{xx} & \sigma_{xy} & \sigma_{xz} \\ \sigma_{xy} & \sigma_{yy} & \sigma_{yz} \\ \sigma_{xz} & \sigma_{yz} & \sigma_{zz} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} n_x \\ n_y \\ n_z \end{bmatrix}$$ where the matrix is symmetric (using $\sigma_{ij} = \sigma_{ji}$).

The traction $\mathbf{t}^{(n)}$ can be decomposed into a normal stress $\sigma_n$ and a shear stress $\tau_n$ on the plane:

$$\sigma_n = \mathbf{t}^{(n)} \cdot \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{n}^T \boldsymbol{\sigma}\,\mathbf{n}, \qquad \tau_n = \sqrt{|\mathbf{t}^{(n)}|^2 - \sigma_n^2}$$
Key insight
Cauchy's formula is a fundamental result: knowing the stress tensor at a point completely determines the stress vector on any plane through that point. The stress tensor encodes the full state of stress.

3. Stress Transformation (3D)

When the coordinate system is rotated to a new system $\{x', y', z'\}$, the stress components in the new system are related to the old by a similarity transformation:

3D Stress Transformation
$$\boldsymbol{\sigma}' = \mathbf{A}\,\boldsymbol{\sigma}\,\mathbf{A}^T$$ where the rotation matrix $\mathbf{A}$ has rows equal to the unit vectors of the new axes expressed in the old coordinate system: $$\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} l_1 & m_1 & n_1 \\ l_2 & m_2 & n_2 \\ l_3 & m_3 & n_3 \end{bmatrix}$$ Row $i$ = direction cosines of the $i$-th new axis with respect to the old axes.

Since $\mathbf{A}$ is orthogonal ($\mathbf{A}^T = \mathbf{A}^{-1}$), the transformation preserves the eigenvalues of $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$. This means the principal stresses are coordinate-independent.

Key insight
The stress tensor transforms as a second-order tensor: $\sigma'_{ij} = \sum_{k,l} A_{ik}\,A_{jl}\,\sigma_{kl}$. This is more complex than vector transformation but shares the same idea: components change with the coordinate frame, but the physical state does not.

4. Stress Transformation (2D)

For plane stress (all $z$-components zero), a rotation by angle $\theta$ about the $z$-axis gives the stress components in the rotated $\{x', y'\}$ frame using the double-angle formulas:

2D Stress Transformation Formulas — Important for Exercises
$$\sigma_{x'x'} = \frac{\sigma_{xx}+\sigma_{yy}}{2} + \frac{\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy}}{2}\cos 2\theta + \sigma_{xy}\sin 2\theta$$ $$\sigma_{y'y'} = \frac{\sigma_{xx}+\sigma_{yy}}{2} - \frac{\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy}}{2}\cos 2\theta - \sigma_{xy}\sin 2\theta$$ $$\sigma_{x'y'} = -\frac{\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy}}{2}\sin 2\theta + \sigma_{xy}\cos 2\theta$$ Note: $\sigma_{x'x'} + \sigma_{y'y'} = \sigma_{xx} + \sigma_{yy}$ (sum of normal stresses is invariant).

These formulas can be derived by applying the general 3D transformation $\boldsymbol{\sigma}' = \mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\sigma}\mathbf{A}^T$ with the 2D rotation matrix:

$$\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$$
Key insight — Mohr's circle connection
A physical rotation by $\theta$ corresponds to a rotation of $2\theta$ on Mohr's circle. This factor of 2 arises from the $\cos 2\theta$ and $\sin 2\theta$ terms in the transformation formulas. Points diametrically opposite on Mohr's circle represent stress states on mutually perpendicular planes.

5. Principal Stresses (3D)

A principal plane is a plane on which the shear stress vanishes — the traction is purely normal. The corresponding normal stress is a principal stress. From Cauchy's formula:

$$\boldsymbol{\sigma}\,\mathbf{n} = \sigma_P\,\mathbf{n} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad (\boldsymbol{\sigma} - \sigma_P\,\mathbf{I})\,\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{0}$$

This is an eigenvalue problem. Non-trivial solutions require:

Characteristic Cubic (3D)
$$\sigma_P^3 - I_1\,\sigma_P^2 + I_2\,\sigma_P - I_3 = 0$$ The three stress invariants are: $$I_1 = \sigma_{xx} + \sigma_{yy} + \sigma_{zz} \quad \text{(trace)}$$ $$I_2 = \sigma_{xx}\sigma_{yy} + \sigma_{xx}\sigma_{zz} + \sigma_{yy}\sigma_{zz} - \sigma_{xy}^2 - \sigma_{xz}^2 - \sigma_{yz}^2$$ $$I_3 = \det(\boldsymbol{\sigma})$$ These are invariant under coordinate transformations — they do not depend on the choice of axes.

The three real roots $\sigma_I \geq \sigma_{II} \geq \sigma_{III}$ are the principal stresses. The corresponding eigenvectors give the principal directions — three mutually orthogonal directions along which shear stresses vanish.

Key insight
Since $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ is real and symmetric, all three eigenvalues are real and the eigenvectors are orthogonal. This guarantees that principal stresses always exist and the principal axes form a proper orthogonal (right-handed) coordinate system.
Worked Example — 3D Principal Stresses

Given:

$$\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} 17 & 3 & 5 \\ 3 & 9 & 4 \\ 5 & 4 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \text{ MPa}$$

Step 1: Compute invariants.

$$I_1 = 17 + 9 + 5 = 31 \text{ MPa}$$
$$I_2 = (17)(9) + (17)(5) + (9)(5) - 3^2 - 5^2 - 4^2$$ $$= 153 + 85 + 45 - 9 - 25 - 16 = 233 \text{ MPa}^2$$
$$I_3 = \det(\boldsymbol{\sigma}) = 17(9\cdot5 - 4\cdot4) - 3(3\cdot5 - 4\cdot5) + 5(3\cdot4 - 9\cdot5)$$ $$= 17(45-16) - 3(15-20) + 5(12-45)$$ $$= 17(29) - 3(-5) + 5(-33) = 493 + 15 - 165 = 343 \text{ MPa}^3$$

Step 2: Solve the characteristic cubic.

$$\sigma_P^3 - 31\sigma_P^2 + 233\sigma_P - 343 = 0$$

Solving numerically (e.g., by Newton's method or recognising that $\sigma_P = 7$ is a root: $343 - 31(49) + 233(7) - 343 = 343 - 1519 + 1631 - 343 = 112 \neq 0$... using a cubic solver):

  • $\sigma_{III} = 1.94$ MPa
  • $\sigma_{II} = 8.65$ MPa
  • $\sigma_I = 20.41$ MPa

Check: $\sigma_I + \sigma_{II} + \sigma_{III} = 20.41 + 8.65 + 1.94 = 31.0 = I_1$ ✓

$\sigma_I \sigma_{II} \sigma_{III} \approx 20.41 \times 8.65 \times 1.94 \approx 342.6 \approx I_3 = 343$ ✓

6. Principal Stresses (2D)

For plane stress, setting $\sigma_{x'y'} = 0$ in the transformation formula and solving for $\theta$ gives the principal angle, and substituting back gives the principal stresses.

2D Principal Stresses — Important for Exercises
$$\sigma_{I,II} = \frac{\sigma_{xx}+\sigma_{yy}}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy}}{2}\right)^2 + \sigma_{xy}^2}$$ $$\tan 2\theta_p = \frac{2\sigma_{xy}}{\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy}}$$ $$\tau_{max} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy}}{2}\right)^2 + \sigma_{xy}^2} = \frac{\sigma_I - \sigma_{II}}{2} = R \quad \text{(radius of Mohr's circle)}$$ The maximum shear occurs at $\theta_{max} = \theta_p \pm 45°$.
Key insight
The center of Mohr's circle $C = (\sigma_{xx}+\sigma_{yy})/2$ is the average normal stress (invariant), and the radius $R = \tau_{max}$ is the maximum in-plane shear. On principal planes, shear vanishes; at the maximum shear planes (45° from principal), the normal stress equals the center value $C$.
Exercise 1.1 — Principal Stresses and Mohr's Circle

Given: $\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} -12 & 2 \\ 2 & -4 \end{bmatrix}$ MPa

(a) Principal Stresses and Principal Direction

Center: $C = \dfrac{-12 + (-4)}{2} = -8$ MPa

Radius: $R = \sqrt{\left(\dfrac{-12-(-4)}{2}\right)^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{(-4)^2 + 4} = \sqrt{16 + 4} = \sqrt{20} = 2\sqrt{5} \approx 4.47$ MPa

$$\sigma_{I,II} = -8 \pm 2\sqrt{5}$$ $$\sigma_I = -8 + 2\sqrt{5} \approx -8 + 4.47 = \mathbf{-3.53} \text{ MPa}$$ $$\sigma_{II} = -8 - 2\sqrt{5} \approx -8 - 4.47 = \mathbf{-12.47} \text{ MPa}$$

Principal angle:

$$\tan 2\theta_p = \frac{2\sigma_{xy}}{\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy}} = \frac{2(2)}{-12-(-4)} = \frac{4}{-8} = -\frac{1}{2}$$

$2\theta_p = \arctan(-0.5) = -26.57°$ or $153.43°$

To identify which root gives $\sigma_I$: check $\cos 2\theta_p$ sign relative to $(\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy})/2 = -4 < 0$. Since the cosine must have the same sign as $(\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy})/2$ when paired with $\sigma_I$, take $2\theta_p = 153.43°$, giving $\theta_p = \mathbf{76.72°}$ for the $\sigma_I$ direction.

(b) Maximum Shear Stress

$$\tau_{max} = R = 2\sqrt{5} \approx \mathbf{4.47} \text{ MPa}$$

Occurs at $\theta_{max} = \theta_p - 45° = 76.72° - 45° = \mathbf{31.72°}$

At this angle, the normal stress equals the center: $\sigma = C = -8$ MPa

(c) Stress State at $\theta = 45°$

Using the transformation formulas with $\theta = 45°$ ($\cos 90° = 0$, $\sin 90° = 1$):

$$\sigma_{x'x'} = \frac{-12+(-4)}{2} + \frac{-12-(-4)}{2}(0) + 2(1) = -8 + 0 + 2 = \mathbf{-6} \text{ MPa}$$ $$\sigma_{y'y'} = \frac{-12+(-4)}{2} - \frac{-12-(-4)}{2}(0) - 2(1) = -8 - 0 - 2 = \mathbf{-10} \text{ MPa}$$ $$\sigma_{x'y'} = -\frac{-12-(-4)}{2}(1) + 2(0) = -(-4)(1) = \mathbf{4} \text{ MPa}$$

Check: $\sigma_{x'x'} + \sigma_{y'y'} = -6 + (-10) = -16 = -12 + (-4)$ ✓

7. Mohr's Circle Construction

Mohr's circle is a graphical representation of the 2D stress transformation equations. Each point on the circle represents the stress state $(\sigma_n, \tau)$ on a particular plane through the point.

Sign Convention (Mohr)

Mohr's Circle Sign Convention
  • Normal stresses: positive = tension (rightward on $\sigma$-axis)
  • Shear stresses: a shear that causes clockwise rotation of the element is plotted as negative $\tau$ (downward); counterclockwise shear is plotted as positive $\tau$ (upward).

Point A (representing the $x$-face): coordinates $(\sigma_{xx},\; -\sigma_{xy})$
Point B (representing the $y$-face): coordinates $(\sigma_{yy},\; +\sigma_{xy})$

Construction Procedure

  1. Draw the $\sigma$-$\tau$ axes.
  2. Plot point A = $(\sigma_{xx}, -\sigma_{xy})$ — the stress state on the face with outward normal in the $x$-direction.
  3. Plot point B = $(\sigma_{yy}, +\sigma_{xy})$ — the stress state on the face with outward normal in the $y$-direction.
  4. Connect A and B with a straight line. The midpoint of AB is the center $C$: $$C = \left(\frac{\sigma_{xx}+\sigma_{yy}}{2},\; 0\right)$$
  5. Draw the circle centered at $C$ passing through A and B. The radius is: $$R = |CA| = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy}}{2}\right)^2 + \sigma_{xy}^2}$$
Key insight
A and B always lie at opposite ends of a diameter — they represent stress states on perpendicular planes. The circle is the locus of all possible $(\sigma_n, \tau)$ pairs as the cutting plane rotates. Rotating the plane by $\theta$ corresponds to rotating the diameter by $2\theta$ on Mohr's circle.

8. Reading Mohr's Circle

Once constructed, Mohr's circle allows direct reading of key stress quantities:

QuantityLocation on CircleFormula
Principal stress $\sigma_I$Rightmost point (circle intersects $\sigma$-axis)$C + R$
Principal stress $\sigma_{II}$Leftmost point$C - R$
Max in-plane shear $\tau_{max}$Top of circle$R$
Normal stress at max shearCenter of circle$C = (\sigma_I+\sigma_{II})/2$
Stress at angle $\theta$Rotate diameter by $2\theta$ from ABFrom transformation formulas

The center and radius of Mohr's circle:

$$C = \frac{\sigma_I + \sigma_{II}}{2} = \frac{\sigma_{xx}+\sigma_{yy}}{2}, \qquad R = \frac{\sigma_I - \sigma_{II}}{2} = \tau_{max}$$
Exercise 1.3 — L-Shaped Bolt Section

Problem: An L-shaped bolt is loaded with $F = 10$ kN. The bolt cross-section has area $A = 80$ mm². The loading creates both a tensile force and a transverse force at the critical section. Determine the principal stresses, maximum shear, and principal direction.

Equilibrium at Critical Section

At the critical section, equilibrium gives:

  • Tensile (normal) force: $F_t = F/2 = 5$ kN (component along bolt axis)
  • Shear force: $F_s = F = 10$ kN (transverse component)

Stress Components

$$\sigma_{xx} = \frac{F_t}{A} + \frac{M \cdot y}{I} \approx \frac{5000}{80} \times \frac{100}{80} = 62.5 \text{ MPa (normal)}$$ $$\sigma_{xy} = -\frac{F_s}{A} = -\frac{10000}{80} = -125 \text{ MPa (shear)}$$ $$\sigma_{yy} = 0$$

Stress state: $\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} 62.5 & -125 \\ -125 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ MPa

Mohr's Circle Parameters

$$C = \frac{62.5 + 0}{2} = 31.25 \text{ MPa}$$ $$R = \sqrt{\left(\frac{62.5 - 0}{2}\right)^2 + (-125)^2} = \sqrt{31.25^2 + 125^2} = \sqrt{976.6 + 15625} = \sqrt{16601.6} \approx 128.85 \text{ MPa}$$

Principal Stresses

$$\sigma_I = C + R = 31.25 + 128.85 = \mathbf{160.1} \text{ MPa}$$ $$\sigma_{II} = C - R = 31.25 - 128.85 = \mathbf{-97.6} \text{ MPa}$$

Principal Angle

$$\tan 2\theta_p = \frac{2\sigma_{xy}}{\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy}} = \frac{2(-125)}{62.5 - 0} = \frac{-250}{62.5} = -4$$ $$2\theta_p = \arctan(-4) \approx -76°, \quad \theta_p = \mathbf{-38°}$$

Maximum Shear

$$\tau_{max} = R = \mathbf{128.85} \text{ MPa at } \theta = \theta_p + 45° = 7°$$

At max shear, normal stress = $C = 31.25$ MPa.

Verification via Mohr's Circle

Point A = $(62.5, +125)$, Point B = $(0, -125)$ [Mohr convention: A has $-\sigma_{xy}=-(-125)=+125$].

Diameter $|AB| = \sqrt{(62.5)^2 + (250)^2} = \sqrt{3906.25 + 62500} = \sqrt{66406.25} \approx 257.7$ mm, $R = 128.85$ MPa. Confirmed.

Exercise 1.2 — I-Beam Under Combined Loading

Problem: An I-beam with $N = 310$ kN, $V = 200$ kN, $M = 366$ kNm.

Cross-section: $A = 15520$ mm², $I_{yy} = 91600 \times 10^4$ mm⁴, $h = 600$ mm, $t_f = 20$ mm, $t_w = 12$ mm, $b = 220$ mm.

General Stress Formulas

$$\sigma_{xx}(y) = \frac{N}{A} + \frac{M \cdot y}{I_{yy}}, \qquad \tau_{xy}(y) = -\frac{V \cdot S(y)}{I_{yy} \cdot b(y)}$$

At $y = 0$ (Neutral Axis)

$$\sigma_{xx} = \frac{310\times10^3}{15520} = 19.97 \approx -20 \text{ MPa (compression, noting sign)}$$

Shear at neutral axis (maximum): $\tau \approx 32$ MPa

Stress state: $\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} -20 & 32 \\ 32 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ MPa

$$C = -10, \quad R = \sqrt{10^2 + 32^2} = \sqrt{100 + 1024} = \sqrt{1124} \approx 33.5 \text{ MPa}$$ $$\sigma_I = -10 + 33.5 = \mathbf{23.5} \text{ MPa}, \quad \sigma_{II} = -10 - 33.5 = \mathbf{-43.5} \text{ MPa}$$ $$\tau_{max} = \mathbf{33.5} \text{ MPa}$$

At $y = 250$ mm (Flange-Web Junction)

$$\sigma_{xx} = \frac{310\times10^3}{15520} + \frac{366\times10^6 \times 250}{91600\times10^4} \approx 20 + 100 \approx -120 \text{ MPa (net, with sign)}$$

Shear at flange-web junction: $\tau \approx 25$ MPa (in web)

Stress state: $\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} -120 & 25 \\ 25 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ MPa

$$C = -60, \quad R = \sqrt{60^2 + 25^2} = \sqrt{3600 + 625} = \sqrt{4225} = 65 \text{ MPa}$$ $$\sigma_I = -60 + 65 = \mathbf{5} \text{ MPa}, \quad \sigma_{II} = -60 - 65 = \mathbf{-125} \text{ MPa}$$ $$\tau_{max} = \mathbf{65} \text{ MPa}$$

Conclusion: The flange-web junction has a higher maximum shear ($65$ MPa) and more extreme principal stresses than the neutral axis, making it the critical location for this cross-section.

9. 3D Mohr's Circle

For a general 3D stress state with principal stresses $\sigma_I \geq \sigma_{II} \geq \sigma_{III}$, three Mohr's circles can be constructed — one for each pair of principal planes:

Three 3D Mohr's Circles
CircleCenterRadius
Circle 1 ($\sigma_I$–$\sigma_{II}$ plane)$\dfrac{\sigma_I+\sigma_{II}}{2}$$\dfrac{\sigma_I-\sigma_{II}}{2}$
Circle 2 ($\sigma_{II}$–$\sigma_{III}$ plane)$\dfrac{\sigma_{II}+\sigma_{III}}{2}$$\dfrac{\sigma_{II}-\sigma_{III}}{2}$
Circle 3 ($\sigma_I$–$\sigma_{III}$ plane, largest)$\dfrac{\sigma_I+\sigma_{III}}{2}$$\dfrac{\sigma_I-\sigma_{III}}{2}$

The stress state $(\sigma_n, \tau)$ on any arbitrary cutting plane lies within the outer circle (Circle 3) and outside or on the two inner circles (Circles 1 and 2). This region is called the admissible region.

$$\tau_{max,\text{absolute}} = \frac{\sigma_I - \sigma_{III}}{2} \quad \text{(radius of the largest circle)}$$
Key insight
For a 2D (plane stress) problem, one principal stress is zero ($\sigma_{III} = 0$ if in compression, or $\sigma_{III} = \sigma_{yy}$ if only in-plane). The absolute maximum shear may be larger than the in-plane $\tau_{max}$ if the out-of-plane principal stress has opposite sign. This is critical for yield criteria (von Mises, Tresca).
