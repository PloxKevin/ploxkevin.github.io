1. Classical vs Buckling Analysis

The fundamental departure between classical beam theory and buckling analysis lies in the geometry used to write equilibrium.

ApproachEquilibrium onResult
Classical beam theoryUndeformed geometryLinear equations, unique solution for any load
Buckling analysisDeformed geometryEigenvalue problem — critical loads $P_C$
Definition: Buckling
Buckling is a sudden large deformation that occurs at the critical load $P_C$, which is typically far below the strength collapse load $P_u = \sigma_u A$. This is a stability phenomenon, not a strength phenomenon. The structure has sufficient material strength but loses its ability to maintain equilibrium in the original configuration.
Key insight
The key departure from classical theory is writing equilibrium with respect to the deformed configuration. This introduces the displacement $w(x)$ into the moment equation, turning the equilibrium condition into a differential equation whose solution reveals the critical loads.

2. Ideal Column (Pin-Pin)

We derive the governing ODE and critical loads for an ideal pin-pin column of length $l$, axial compressive load $P$, and flexural stiffness $EI$.

3. Buckling Modes

The mode shapes for a pin-pin column are:

Buckling Mode Shapes
$$w_n(x) = B\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{l}\right), \qquad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$ Each mode has an effective length $l_k = l/n$, and the critical load can be rewritten as: $$P_n = \frac{\pi^2 EI}{l_k^2}$$

The first mode ($n=1$) gives the lowest critical load — this is the one that governs in practice. Higher modes require restraints to prevent the lower modes.

Applied Example — Steel Bar: Buckling vs Strength

Problem: Steel circular bar, diameter $d = 10$ mm, length $l = 3$ m, $E = 208{,}000$ MPa, $\sigma_u = 360$ MPa.

Cross-Sectional Properties

$$A = \frac{\pi d^2}{4} = \frac{\pi \times 10^2}{4} = 78.5 \text{ mm}^2$$
$$I = \frac{\pi d^4}{64} = \frac{\pi \times 10^4}{64} = 491 \text{ mm}^4$$

Strength Collapse Load

$$P_u = \sigma_u \cdot A = 360 \times 78.5 = 28{,}260 \text{ N}$$

Euler Buckling Load

$$P_E = \frac{\pi^2 EI}{l^2} = \frac{\pi^2 \times 208{,}000 \times 491}{3000^2} = \frac{1.007 \times 10^9}{9 \times 10^6} \approx 112 \text{ N}$$

Conclusion

The Euler buckling load (112 N) is 252 times lower than the strength collapse load (28,260 N). The bar buckles elastically long before the material yields.

Key insight
Slender bars fail by buckling, not yielding. The slenderness ratio $\lambda = l/r_i$ governs which failure mode controls. For $\lambda$ large enough, buckling is catastrophic and occurs at a tiny fraction of the strength capacity.

4. Boundary Conditions & Effective Length

Different boundary conditions change the shape of the buckled column, and hence the critical load. The general formula is:

General Critical Load Formula
$$P_C = \frac{\pi^2 EI}{l_k^2}, \qquad l_k = k_e \cdot l$$ where $l_k$ is the effective length and $k_e$ is the effective length factor.
Definition: Flexural Point
A flexural point (point of contraflexure) is a point of zero bending moment in the buckled shape. The distance between two adjacent flexural points equals the effective length $l_k$.
Boundary Condition $k_e$ $P_C$ relative to $P_E$ Description
Cantilever (fixed-free) 2 $P_E / 4$ Free top, fixed bottom
Pin-Pin 1 $P_E$ Reference case
Pin-Fixed 0.7 $\approx 2.0\,P_E$ One end pinned, one fixed
Fixed-Fixed 0.5 $4\,P_E$ Both ends fully fixed
Derivation — Pin-Fixed Column: Transcendental Equation

For a column pinned at the bottom and fixed at the top, the fixed end exerts a horizontal reaction $R$. The moment at position $x$ from the pin:

$$M(x) = -P\,w(x) + R(l - x)$$

Combined with $M = EI\,w''$:

$$w'' + \alpha^2 w = \frac{R}{EI}(l - x), \qquad \alpha^2 = \frac{P}{EI}$$

General Solution

$$w(x) = A\cos(\alpha x) + B\sin(\alpha x) + \frac{R}{P}(l - x)$$

Boundary Conditions

  • $w(0) = 0$: $A + \frac{Rl}{P} = 0 \Rightarrow A = -\frac{Rl}{P}$
  • $w'(l) = 0$ (fixed end, no rotation): $B\alpha\cos(\alpha l) - A\alpha\sin(\alpha l) - \frac{R}{P} = 0$
  • $w(l) = 0$: $A\cos(\alpha l) + B\sin(\alpha l) = 0 \Rightarrow B = -A\frac{\cos(\alpha l)}{\sin(\alpha l)}$

Transcendental Equation

Combining the boundary conditions leads to the characteristic equation:

$$\tan(\alpha l) = \alpha l$$

The smallest non-trivial solution is $\alpha l = 4.493$, giving:

$$k_e = \frac{\pi}{\alpha l} = \frac{\pi}{4.493} \approx 0.699 \approx 0.7$$

Hence $l_k = 0.7\,l$ and $P_C = \pi^2 EI / (0.7l)^2 \approx 2.04\,P_E$.

5. Frame Buckling

In frames, columns are not isolated: the beam stiffness influences how the column ends are restrained, and hence determines $l_k$. The extreme case method provides practical bounds.

Extreme Case Method
For a column in a frame, bound the effective length by considering two limiting cases:
  • $EI_{\text{beam}} \to \infty$: beam is infinitely stiff → column end is fully fixed
  • $EI_{\text{beam}} = 0$: beam has zero stiffness → column end is pinned
The real $l_k$ lies between these two bounds.
Frame Example — Two Columns + Connecting Beam

Structure: Two identical columns (flexural stiffness $EI_2$, height $h$) connected at the top by a beam (flexural stiffness $EI_1$). Columns are pinned at the base.

Limiting Cases

Case 1: $EI_1 \to \infty$ (rigid beam)

The top of each column cannot rotate. The column is effectively pin-fixed: $k_e = 0.7$, so $l_k = 0.7\,h$.

$$P_{C,\text{upper}} = \frac{\pi^2 EI_2}{(0.7h)^2} \approx \frac{2.04\,\pi^2 EI_2}{h^2}$$

Case 2: $EI_1 = 0$ (no beam)

The top of each column is free to rotate. The column is pin-pin: $k_e = 1.0$, so $l_k = h$.

$$P_{C,\text{lower}} = \frac{\pi^2 EI_2}{h^2}$$

Real Case

For any finite $EI_1 > 0$:

$$0.7\,h < l_k < h, \qquad P_{C,\text{lower}} < P_C < P_{C,\text{upper}}$$

A more precise value requires solving the frame's eigenvalue problem, but the bounds are useful for quick design checks.

Key insight
Increasing beam stiffness (relative to column stiffness) always reduces the effective length and increases the critical load. Sway frames (columns allowed to sway sideways) have higher $k_e$ values than non-sway frames.

6. Slenderness & Collapse Curve

Radius of Gyration and Slenderness

Key Definitions

Radius of gyration: $r_i^2 = I/A$, so $r_i = \sqrt{I/A}$.

Slenderness ratio: $\lambda = l_k / r_i$ (dimensionless measure of column slenderness).

Euler buckling stress:

$$\sigma_E = \frac{P_E}{A} = E\left(\frac{\pi}{\lambda}\right)^2$$

Relative slenderness:

$$\lambda_r = \frac{\lambda}{\pi}\sqrt{\frac{\sigma_y}{E}}$$ This compares buckling stress to yield stress. $\lambda_r = 1$ marks the transition.

Collapse Curve

The normalized collapse stress $\sigma_C / \sigma_y$ as a function of $\lambda_r$:

$$\frac{\sigma_E}{\sigma_y} = \frac{1}{\lambda_r^2}$$
RegimeConditionGoverning failure$\sigma_C$
Short/stocky $\lambda_r < 1$ Strength (yielding) $\sigma_C = \sigma_y$
Slender $\lambda_r > 1$ Buckling (Euler) $\sigma_C = \sigma_y / \lambda_r^2 = \pi^2 E / \lambda^2$
Key insight
At $\lambda_r = 1$, the Euler buckling stress equals the yield stress. For $\lambda_r > 1$, the column buckles elastically (Euler range). For $\lambda_r < 1$, the column fails plastically. Practical design curves (Tetmeyer, Johnson) are empirical corrections that account for initial imperfections and residual stresses, lying below the theoretical Euler curve.

7. Combined Axial Force + Bending

Eccentric Compression — Cantilever

Consider a cantilever column of length $l$, with an axial compressive force $P$ applied with eccentricity $e$ at the free end. Let $\delta$ be the tip deflection. The ODE for the deflection $w(x)$ is:

ODE: Eccentric Compression on Cantilever
$$w'' + \alpha^2 w = \alpha^2(\delta + e), \qquad \alpha^2 = \frac{P}{EI}$$ General solution: $w(x) = (\delta + e)(1 - \cos\alpha x)$

Tip Deflection

Applying the boundary condition $w(l) = \delta$:

$$\delta = (\delta + e)(1 - \cos\alpha l)$$
$$\delta = \frac{e(1 - \cos\alpha l)}{\cos\alpha l}$$

As $\alpha l \to \pi/2$ (i.e., $P \to P_C = \pi^2 EI / (4l^2)$, the cantilever critical load): $\delta \to \infty$.

Key insight
Eccentricity changes the amplitude of the deflection but does not change the critical load. The same $P_C$ applies regardless of eccentricity $e$. As $P \to P_C$, deflections grow without bound — even for very small $e$.

Maximum Stress — Amplification Factor

The maximum bending moment occurs at the fixed end, amplified by the P-$\delta$ effect:

Maximum Stress (Eccentric Cantilever)
$$\sigma_{max} = \frac{P}{A} + \frac{P\,e\,y_{max}}{I\cos(\alpha l)}$$ The factor $\dfrac{1}{\cos(\alpha l)}$ is the amplification factor — it accounts for the additional moment due to the P-$\delta$ interaction. As $P \to P_C$, $\cos(\alpha l) \to 0$ and $\sigma_{max} \to \infty$.
Exercise — Simply Supported Column with Lateral UDL and Axial Load

Problem: A simply supported column of length $l$ carries a uniformly distributed lateral load $q$ and an axial compressive load $P$. Derive the maximum bending moment including P-$\delta$ amplification.

Governing ODE

The combined loading gives:

$$EI\,w'' + P\,w = \frac{q}{2}\left(\frac{l^2}{4} - \left(x - \frac{l}{2}\right)^2\right)$$

Or, with $\alpha^2 = P/EI$:

$$w'' + \alpha^2 w = \frac{q}{2EI}\left(\frac{l^2}{4} - \left(x - \frac{l}{2}\right)^2\right)$$

Solution

The particular solution (using the series expansion of $1/\cos$ for small $\alpha l$) leads to a midspan deflection amplified relative to the classical result $w_0 = 5ql^4/(384EI)$. More exactly, the maximum midspan deflection is:

$$w_{max} = \frac{5ql^4}{384\,EI} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - P/P_E} \quad \text{(approximate)}$$

Maximum Bending Moment

The primary moment from $q$ alone at midspan is $M_0 = ql^2/8$. With the P-$\delta$ amplification:

$$M_{max} = \frac{ql^2}{8} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - P/P_E}$$

This approximate formula shows that the moment is amplified by the factor $1/(1-P/P_E)$. When $P \to P_E$, $M_{max} \to \infty$ — reflecting the instability.

Physical Interpretation

The lateral load $q$ causes initial deflection, which generates additional moment from $P$, which causes more deflection, and so on. This positive feedback loop is the essence of P-$\delta$ effects. The amplification factor $1/(1-P/P_E)$ emerges from the geometric series summing all these contributions.

8. Design Criteria

In practical design, a buckling coefficient $\gamma$ reduces the allowable compressive stress to account for the risk of buckling.

Buckling Coefficient
$$\gamma(\lambda) = \frac{\sigma_c}{\sigma_u} \le 1$$ where $\sigma_c$ is the compressive strength reduced for buckling and $\sigma_u$ is the ultimate material strength. $\gamma = 1$ for stocky columns (no buckling reduction); $\gamma \to 0$ for very slender columns.

Design Interaction Formula

When a column carries both axial force $P$ and bending moment $M$, the design criterion is:

Combined Axial + Bending Design Check
$$\frac{P}{A\,\gamma\,\sigma_u} + \frac{M\,y_{max}}{I\,\sigma_u} \le 1$$ Note: $\gamma$ only reduces the capacity in the axial force term. When bending dominates, the interaction is governed primarily by the bending term.

Empirical Buckling Curves

Three classical empirical collapse curve models:

ModelFormulaApplicability
Tetmeyer (linear) $\sigma_c = \sigma_u(a - b\lambda)$ Intermediate slenderness; fits experimental data
Johnson (parabolic) $\sigma_c = \sigma_u\!\left(1 - \frac{\sigma_u \lambda^2}{4\pi^2 E}\right)$ Moderate slenderness; tangent to Euler at transition
Euler (hyperbolic) $\sigma_c = \dfrac{\pi^2 E}{\lambda^2}$ High slenderness ($\lambda_r > 1$); elastic buckling
Key insight
The interaction formula is linear in the two utilization ratios (axial and bending), but it is conservative: the actual interaction surface is typically convex. The buckling coefficient $\gamma$ is read from design curves (e.g., EN 1993 for steel) that incorporate initial imperfections and residual stresses.
